Timeline

A general overview of major historical dates regarding fire supression in the US.

Late 1800s

Large fires were occuring that sparked conversation within the conservation community feating that timber supplies would be decreasing. This lead to setting aside land to be set aside as national forest reseervation to protect timber supplies.

1905

The United States Forest Service was created and was given control over the land that was set aside in the late 1890s.

1910

Three massive fires known as the Big Blowup occured burning over 3 million acres in two days. This lead to fire administrators meeting and they convinced themselve that total fire supression was the only to prevent another event like this from happening again. As a result the Forest Serive put this polciy in place. Three of the men who fought the Big Blowup fires had high postions in the Forest Services which allowed for the total fire supression policy to easily be enacted.

1911

The Weeks Act of 1911 was past which was legistlation between the federal and state goverment for cooperative firefighting that offered finiacal incentive for the states for fighting fires.

1933

The federal government created the Civilian Conservation Corps whcih had thousands of works building fire breaks and fighting fires.

1935

The 10 a.m. policy was created which said that every fire needed to be supressed by 10 a.m. the day after it was reported.

1944

Smokey the Bear was created by the US government.

1960s

Significant amount of research started to come out stating the postive role that fire has in forest ecosystems.

1970s

The research from the 60s made a radical shift in fire policy. Fires were aloud to brun when and where the government deemed approiate.

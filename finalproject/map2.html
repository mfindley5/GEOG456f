California Wildfire History

Intro

Fires are naturally occuring and often needed for the survival of various ecosystems. Humans often view fire as a bad thing for a variety of reasons. This can lead to policies of complete fire suppression. This website will give a large amount of information regarding the history of wildfire suppression and the state of wildfires currently in California. To understand the why complete fire suppression can be so bad, people need to know why fires can be benefical. First, it helps clean the forest floor and opens it up to sunlight. Also, fires will burn the brush from the floor allowing for low intensity fires that are mostly on the ground. Fires also help provide habitats by clearing the heavy brush away so there can be new grasses and shrubs that provide food and habitat for many species. Fires can also help kill various diseases that would kill species. Another major aspect of fire is that some species are dependent on fire to reproduce. Some species require fire every 3-25 years to reproduce and others have fire resistant bark and cones that need heat to be able to open and then regenerate. While fires may look intimidating or scary they are quite beneficial and needed for the success of some ecosystems

Wildfire Suppression Timeline

A general overview of major historical dates regarding fire supression in the US.

Late 1800s

Large fires were occuring that sparked conversation within the conservation community feating that timber supplies would be decreasing. This lead to setting aside land to be set aside as national forest reseervation to protect timber supplies.

1905

The United States Forest Service was created and was given control over the land that was set aside in the late 1890s.

1910

Three massive fires known as the Big Blowup occured burning over 3 million acres in two days. This lead to fire administrators meeting and they convinced themselve that total fire supression was the only to prevent another event like this from happening again. As a result the Forest Serive put this polciy in place. Three of the men who fought the Big Blowup fires had high postions in the Forest Services which allowed for the total fire supression policy to easily be enacted.

1911

The Weeks Act of 1911 was past which was legistlation between the federal and state goverment for cooperative firefighting that offered finiacal incentive for the states for fighting fires.

1926

The US Forest Service enacted a polciy the required all fires over 10 acres to be suppresed and put out.

1933

The federal government created the Civilian Conservation Corps whcih had thousands of works building fire breaks and fighting fires.

1935

The 10 a.m. policy was created which said that every fire needed to be supressed by 10 a.m. the day after it was reported. This replaced the policy in 1926 that allowed fires under 10 acres to burn.

1944

Smokey the Bear was created by the US government. ADD MORE INFO ABOUT SMOKEY

1960s

Significant amount of research started to come out stating the postive role that fire has in forest ecosystems.

1970s

The research from the 60s made a radical shift in fire policy. Fires were aloud to burn when and where the government deemed appropriate. This got rid of the 10 a.m. policy.
